3 Terms and definitions
T-35

3.1 Jurisdictional terms
T-45

3.1.1 campus

jurisdictional area (3.1.4) whose jurisdictional entity (3.1.6) is the owner of the area, with the exception of government-owned, transport-related areas

Note 1 to entry: A campus is a site typified by a school, hospital, store, or some other set of one or more buildings and might include parking or other transport facilities where the owner of the campus has the jurisdictional authority to establish its own rules. The rules can be in lieu of or in addition to rules established by the representative governmental entities.

EXAMPLE  A government-owned university is a campus
T-123

3.1.2 governmental area

jurisdictional area (3.1.4) whose jurisdictional entity (3.1.6) is a governmental authority and the facility is part of the jurisdiction's transport network

EXAMPLE  A subway station is a governmental area
T-46

3.1.3 jurisdiction

jurisdictional authority

legal power to issue rules of behaviour for a defined geographical territory
T-47

3.1.4 jurisdictional area

territory for which a jurisdictional entity (3.1.6) can issue rules (3.x.x)

Note 1 to entry: A jurisdictional area might include non-contiguous locations.
T-48

3.1.5 jurisdictional boundary

territorial limit associated with a jurisdiction (3.1.3)

 Note 1 to entry:           A geofence can be used to electronically represent a jurisdictional boundary.
T-31

3.1.6 jurisdictional entity

person (ISO 14812, 3.1.1.6) or organization who has jurisdiction (3.1.3)

EXAMPLE               A city government

Note 1 to entry:   In the case of a geopolitical jurisdiction, the jurisdictional entity is typically the recognized government for the territory; in the case of a campus, the jurisdictional entity is typically the landowner. 

Note 2 to entry: A jurisdictional entity assigns its authority to one or more regulators
  •  Notes:

    Example: A city might have the city council assigned as the primary regulator. The city council could then establish the police department and traffic department as additional regulators for the same jurisdiction. All of the regulators would have the authority to establish rules while only the city council would have the authority to designate additional regulators.
T-116

3.1.7 METR coverage area

sub-area which supports at least some METR data (3.x.x)
T-121

3.1.8 sub-area

geographic area entirely within a jurisdictional area that has a consistent level of support for METR data throughout
T-122

3.1.9 non-covered area

sub-area for which METR data is not available

EXAMPLE  Speed limit regulations for motor vehicles within a specified jurisdictional area

T-11

3.2.1.5 rule

regulation, advisory, or guidance established by a regulatory agency for its jurisdictional area

Note 1 to entry: The jurisdictional area can be a campus.

Note 2 to entry: The primary focus of METR is to provide rules that are likely to impact the dynamic driving task (DDT, ISO 14812:2021, 3.7.3.1). The exact boundary of which rules are a part of METR is left to those deploying METR distribution systems.

3.3.1.3 regulatory agency

organization authorized by a jurisdictional entity (3.1.6) to fulfil the regulator (3.3.1.2) role

EXAMPLE 1           road authority for a city

EXAMPLE 2           city council for the city

Note 1 to entry:   A jurisdictional entity often authorizes multiple regulatory agencies to fulfil parts of its regulatory authority. For example, the city council might be responsible for legislative regulations (e.g., default speed limit on city streets) while the road authority might be responsible for warrant-based regulations (e.g., posted speed limit on a specific street). For small jurisdictional areas, the jurisdictional entity and regulatory agency might represent the same organization or person.

Note 2 to entry: A regulatory agency can perform other roles as well.

Note 1 to entry: The expected rules disseminator role is primarily envisioned to be fulfiled by a central system, but can be fulfiled by a mobile unit dispatched to a location without internet connectivity.

Note 2 to entry: An expected rules disseminator can also distribute unexpected rules, but does not bear the responsibility for doing so.
