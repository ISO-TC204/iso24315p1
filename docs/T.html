3 Terms and definitions
T-35

3.1 Jurisdictional terms
T-45

3.1.1 campus

jurisdictional area (3.1.x) that is not managed as a part of the regional transport network

Note 1 to entry: A campus is a site typified by a school, hospital, store, or some other set of one or more buildings and might include parking or other transport facilities where the owner of the campus has jurisdiction rather than territorial government
T-46

3.1.2 jurisdiction

jurisdictional authority

legal power to issue rules of behaviour for a defined geographical territory
T-47

3.1.3 jurisdictional area

territory for which a jurisdictional entity (3.1.x) can issue rules of behaviour

Note 1 to entry: A jurisdictional area might include non-contiguous locations.
T-48

3.1.4 jurisdictional boundary

territorial limit associated with a jurisdiction (3.x.x)

 Note 1 to entry:           A geofence can be used to electronically represent a jurisdictional boundary.
T-31

3.1.5 jurisdictional entity

person (ISO 14812, x.x) or organization who has jurisdiction (3.x.x)

EXAMPLE               A city government

Note 1 to entry:   In the case of a geopolitical jurisdiction, the jurisdictional entity is typically the recognized government for the territory; in the case of a campus, the jurisdictional entity is typically the landowner. 

Note 2 to entry: A jurisdictional entity assigns its authority to one or more regulators
  •  Notes:

    Example: A city might have the city council assigned as the primary regulator. The city council could then establish the police department and traffic department as additional regulators for the same jurisdiction. All of the regulators would have the authority to establish rules while only the city council would have the authority to designate additional regulators.
T-116

3.1.6 METR management area

geofenced area used to identify conformance to the various METR information categories

T-39

3.2.1 Activation terms
T-51

3.2.1.1 condition-based

being active based on explicitly stated conditions other than time-based

EXAMPLE               “when workers present”, “dusk”, “in winter”
T-50

3.2.1.2 conditional

being active based on condition(s) explicitly defined and posted in advance

Note 1 to entry:   Conditional logic is typically based on a single condition but might be based on multiple conditions.
T-52

3.2.1.3 needs-based

being active based on conditions not explicitly stated in advance

Note 1 to entry:   Needs-based official notices are typically activated by operations personnel

Note 2 to entry:   Needs-based official notices are typically activated based on emergent needs

EXAMPLE               the closure of a road due to snow conditions
T-53

3.2.1.4 on-going

being active during the entire validity period, unless overridden

EXAMPLE               A static “do not enter” regulation

T-54

3.2.2.1 enacted

put into effect
T-55

3.2.2.2 extemporaneous

publicized (x.x) with an enactment time prior to the expiration of a relevant downloaded rule set

Note 1 to entry:   Ad-hoc rules are typically made in response to current conditions.

Note 2 to entry:   Ad-hoc rules are typically temporary.

Note 3 to entry:   Ad-hoc rules typically require notifications involving electronic communications to travellers (e.g., radio, connected vehicle), remote control of electronic roadside devices (e.g., variable message signs), or deployment of physical resources (e.g., signs immediately deployed, field personnel directing traffic).
T-56

3.2.2.3 premeditated

publicized (x.x) with an enactment time after the expiration of all relevant downloaded rule sets

Note 1 to entry:   Premeditated rules are often posted prior to becoming valid.

3.2.5 Validity terms
T-63

3.2.5.1 active

current (x.x) and presently in effect

Note 1 to entry:   An official notice might only be in effect during certain hours of the day.

Note 2 to entry:   An active regulation can be enforced

Note 3 to entry:   Active guidance reflects current conditions, e.g., detours might be planned before they are put into effect.
T-64

3.2.5.2 current

having an inception (x.x) in the past and a termination (x.x) either undefined or in the future

Note 1 to entry:   A current official notice might be active or inactive based on causality parameters (See 3.x.x.x through 3.x.x.x)
T-13

3.2.5.3 expiration

applies to rule status of the electronic version of a rule that has been downloaded

3.2.5.4 in effect
T-65

3.2.5.5 inactive

current (x.x) but not presently in effect

Note 1 to entry:   An official notice might not be in effect during certain hours of the day.

Note 2 to entry:   A regulation cannot be enforced while it is inactive.

Note 3 to entry:   Guidance information that is not applicable at certain times (e.g., detour guidance associated with road works activity) might be inactive at certain times.
T-120

3.2.5.6 instantiated
T-66

3.2.5.7 inception

<METR> epoch at which an official notice (x.x) became or is expected to become valid (x.x)

EXAMPLE               A daily parking restriction that is intended to be in effect daily from 07:00 to 19:00 and starting 1 July would typically be associated with an inception time of 07:00 on 1 July.
T-67

3.2.5.8 override

to temporarily nullify and replace

Note 1 to entry: It is expected that an override will eventually terminate, even if the expected termination epoch is unknown.

Note 2 to entry:   The item that is temporarily nullified is termed to be “overridden” and the item that it is replaced with is termed to be “overriding”.

Note 3 to entry:   The replacement can be null.
T-68

3.2.5.9 permanent

having an expected termination of never
T-69

3.2.5.10 approved

having an inception in the future
T-70

3.2.5.11 rescinded

having a termination in the past
T-71

3.2.5.12 revoked

having been identified never being valid

Note 1 to entry:   A notice that is revoked indicates that the notice was never valid as declared, although some aspects of the notice might have been valid

EXAMPLE               An electronic no parking regulation might be revoked if it is discovered that the electronic version incorrectly identified the applicable times to be 03:00 to 20:00 when the actual regulation identifies the applicable times to be 08:00 to 20:00.
T-72

3.2.5.13 termination

<METR> epoch at which an official notice (x.x) ceased to be or is expected end being valid (x.x)

EXAMPLE               A daily parking restriction that is intended to be in effect daily from 07:00 to 19:00 and ending 31 July would typically be associated with a termination time of 19:00 on 31 July.
T-12

3.2.5.14 valid

representing trustworthy information about present conditions

EXAMPLE               A daily parking restriction that is intended to be in effect daily from 07:00 to 19:00 on a temporary basis from this 1 July to 31 July would only be valid during the month of July from its inception to its termination (even at night when inactive).

3.3.22 regulator

regulatory agency 

agency appointed by a jurisdictional entity to issue rules (3.x.x) for a jurisdictional area (3.x.x)

EXAMPLE 1           road authority for a city

EXAMPLE 2           city council for the city

Note 1 to entry:   A jurisdictional entity often divides its jurisdictional powers among multiple regulators. For example, the city council might be responsible for legislative regulations while the road authority might be responsible for warrant-based regulations. For small jurisdictional areas, the jurisdictional entity and regulator might represent the same organization or person.

Note 2 to entry: A regulator can perform other roles as well.
